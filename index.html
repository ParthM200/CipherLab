WHY ALL OF THESE FAIL — AND WHAT REPLACED THEM

Every cipher above was called unbreakable at some point. All were broken. The root cause is always the same: either the key space is too small (brute force wins) or statistical patterns survive encryption (frequency analysis wins). Modern cryptography is engineered from the ground up to defeat both.

🔑
AES-256
2001 · Standard today · Used everywhere

Key space: 2²⁵⁶ ≈ 10⁷⁷. If every atom in the observable universe were a computer checking a billion keys per second since the Big Bang, you'd have checked ~10⁴¹ keys — not even close. AES also destroys all statistical patterns through 14 rounds of substitution and permutation. Output is indistinguishable from random noise. Frequency analysis finds nothing.

SYMMETRIC
🔐
RSA
1977 · Public-key cryptography

Based on mathematical asymmetry: multiplying two 1024-bit primes takes milliseconds, but factoring the product is computationally infeasible on classical hardware. Gives you a public key (anyone can encrypt to you) and a private key (only you decrypt). No shared secret needs to be transmitted — which solves the key exchange problem that every classical cipher ignores.

ASYMMETRIC
∞
One-Time Pad
1882 · Theoretically perfect

XOR every bit of the plaintext with a truly random key the same length as the message — use the key exactly once. Claude Shannon proved in 1949 that this provides perfect secrecy: no amount of ciphertext reveals anything about the plaintext, mathematically. Impractical at scale because the key must be as long as the message and securely transferred in advance — but it works. Diplomatic hotlines and nuclear command systems have used it.

PERFECT SECRECY
⚛
Post-Quantum
2024 · NIST standards finalized

Shor's algorithm on a quantum computer breaks RSA and ECC — the factoring problem becomes polynomial-time. Grover's algorithm effectively halves AES key length. NIST finalized the first post-quantum standards in 2024: CRYSTALS-Kyber (key exchange) and CRYSTALS-Dilithium (digital signatures), based on lattice problems that quantum computers cannot shortcut. Encryption is already being re-engineered for a quantum world.
